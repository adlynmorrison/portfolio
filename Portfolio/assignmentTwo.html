There have been many articles written in the case against fast food but recently Michael Moss, a Pulitzer Prize–winning investigative reporter for The Times, wrote an expose on Big Food companies. In detail, Moss explores how the food companies “hyper-engineered, savagely marketed, addiction-creating battle for 'stomach share.'”

The article starts with a secret meeting depicted in Minneapolis, MN Big Food companies in which they were told, under no uncertain terms about obesity rates in correlation to their processed foods that they needed to take responsibility for, and refused in any way to change their ways. The article looks at several foods optimized for consumers such as Dr. Pepper, Lunchables, and Cheetos, sugary, salty, fatty foods that are not good in the quantities American consume them.

Opponents of intense attacks on food companies that will is the ultimate decision behind choosing foods that cause diabetes, obesity, and hypertension. Moss says otherwise. Moss articles points out that people overestimate sheer will power. It flatters egos to believe that they cannot be manipulated, which is largely an illusion. The food companies spend millions of dollars to find the perfect version of a product in “labs and marketing meeting” to get people hooked on foods that are “convenient and inexpensive.” Companies research color to the perfect break point for a potato chip to consumers who, while not powerless, are vulnerable to “industrial formulations and selling campaigns.”

Opponents like A. Barton Hinkle, says the thought of companies trying to please their consumers a sneaky, underhanded thing to do, and therefore morally wrong hilarious. What researchers do is no different from what many people who spend copious amounts of time on their product that is what they are paid for. Companies should spend every waking moment making products better, it is one of the byproducts of free market capitalism.

Some criticizers of the food industry are looking towards the government to make food companies take responsibility for their formulations and their aggressive marketing campaigns. Opponents, however, maintain that it was the government's fault to begin with –starting with corn subsidies that have turned everything into a corn product. The government is also responsible for favoring single-family homes and zoning for big box stores resulting in the suburban sprawl for white Americans and car culture. For blacks left behind in America’s cities, governmental policies resulted in functioning and healthy communities to falter and create the very food deserts Michelle Obama and food activists are trying to reverse.

Redlining was common amongst banks and insurance companies so that individuals and businesses could not receive the financing needed to buy or maintain property. Freeways and eminent domain plowed through areas deemed not worth preserving, ruining businesses and neighborhoods. Policies ensured whole swaths of land where brown people like me lived would not see investment dollars, would not see growth, would not see health, and would not continue to flourish.~ The Boxcar Grocer’s Alphonzo and Alison Cross (Why Mobile is Not Enough)
